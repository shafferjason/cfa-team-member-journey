# Graduation Criteria

## How Graduation Works

Every tier transition follows the same process:

1. **Self-assessment** — The candidate rates themselves on the 4-level scale for each competency at their current tier
2. **Leader assessment** — The evaluating leader rates independently on the same scale
3. **Calibration conversation** — They compare. Gaps become the discussion. Agreement on Level 3 (Consistent) across all competencies = ready.
4. **Operational readiness check** — Evaluating leader confirms all operational skills for the current tier are demonstrated
5. **Final recommendation** — The evaluating leader formally recommends to the next level up
6. **Announcement** — The promotion is communicated to the team with recognition

## Trainer → Team Leader

This is the most important promotion in the organization — the transition from "capable worker who can teach" to "floor leader who sets the tone." The bar is high because the 5 TL Expectations require a specific kind of person.

| Requirement | Threshold |
|------------|-----------|
| Teamwork | Level 3 (Consistent) |
| Caring (deepened) | Level 3 (Consistent) |
| Interpersonal | Level 3 (Consistent) |
| Courage | Level 3 (Consistent) |
| Composure | Level 3 (Consistent) |
| Train-the-Trainer certified | Complete |
| Orientation delivery | Demonstrated |
| Training standards knowledge | Demonstrated |
| Most/all stations on their side | Certified |
| Basic cross-training on other side | Working knowledge |
| Shift management | Demonstrated |
| Cash/register management | Demonstrated |
| Guest recovery | Demonstrated |
| Food safety oversight | Demonstrated |
| Labor awareness | Demonstrated |
| Minimum runway | 12 months (6 as Trainer + 6 in TL development) |
| Recommended by | Director |
| Approved by | Operator |

**Note on runway:** The 12 months includes 6 months as a Trainer (developing Teamwork and Caring) plus additional time developing the Team Leader competencies (Interpersonal, Courage, Composure). The total Trainer → Team Leader transition is 12 months, not 6+12.

**The question being answered:** Can this person lead a shift — staying present, holding the standard, coaching in real time, protecting the guest experience, and keeping the team connected? Do they have the character (Interpersonal, Courage, Composure) to do it consistently, even under pressure?

### What the Director Evaluates

Beyond the competency ratings, the Director assesses readiness through observed behavior across multiple shifts, including high-pressure ones:

**Leadership presence:**
- Does this person lead with awareness, not just activity?
- When they're on shift, does the team perform better?
- Do they delegate tasks so they can lead, or do they get buried in execution?

**Standard-holding:**
- Do they address drift in real time, or let things slide?
- Do they hold the same standard for friends as everyone else?
- Do standards hold during rush, or only when it's calm?

**Character under pressure:**
- When things go wrong, do they respond with a plan or with panic?
- When they receive criticism, do they get defensive or lean in?
- When a hard conversation is needed, do they have it or avoid it?

**Guest protection:**
- Do they step into guest recovery naturally?
- Do they anticipate where the experience might break down?
- Do they take ownership when things go wrong?

**Team connection:**
- Does the team know what they're responsible for on this person's shifts?
- Do they coordinate with other leaders, or operate in isolation?
- Do they know when to own a decision vs. escalate it?

## On-the-Job Tools (Not Graduation Criteria)

The following tools from the Vision & Leadership Directive are used by Team Leaders AFTER they've been promoted — they're daily leadership tools, not graduation requirements.

### Mid-Shift Self-Check (9 Questions)

Team Leaders use these to refocus during a shift:

1. Am I leading right now, or just working?
2. What am I not seeing because I'm heads-down?
3. How does this shift feel for our guests right now? For our team?
4. Is there something slipping that I've been ignoring?
5. Who needs coaching or encouragement that I haven't gotten to?
6. Are we holding the same standards right now as we do when it's calm?
7. Are you happy to be here? Can the team see it on your face?
8. Where is the team struggling the most right now?
9. What can I do to make a change for the betterment of the team at this time?

**How this connects to development:** A Team Leader's ability to honestly and consistently engage with these questions reflects their mastery of the Interpersonal, Courage, and Composure competencies. Directors can use these questions as a coaching framework during 1-on-1s.

### Own It vs. Escalate Framework

**Own It:**
- Guest recovery — resolve quickly with genuine care
- Position adjustments — move people where they're needed
- Real-time coaching — correct, redirect, encourage in the moment
- Maintaining standards — reset the environment, hold the line
- Coordinating with other Team Leaders — stay in sync without waiting for direction

**Escalate:**
- Sending someone home early or adjusting schedules
- Ongoing performance or behavior issues that need documentation
- Guest situations that aren't resolving or are escalating
- Safety concerns or equipment failures
- Anything that could impact the business beyond your shift

**The principle:** When in doubt, handle what you can and loop in your Director. Better to over-communicate than to let something slip.

## Director On-the-Job Expectations

The 5 Director Expectations serve the same purpose for Directors that the TL Expectations serve for Team Leaders — they define what great looks like in the role:

1. **Remove friction by building better systems** — Fix root causes, clarify standards, design workflows, assign process owners
2. **See ahead and prepare the organization** — Forecast needs, set rhythms, spot issues before they hit the shift
3. **Develop leaders who create the experience** — Coach regularly, build independence, place people where they shine, prepare the next group
4. **Shape the culture through key moments** — Show up for openings/transitions/peaks, notice culture drift, model what you expect, guide without taking over
5. **Communicate clearly and create alignment** — Keep all lanes aligned, share expectations that leaders can teach and apply, follow through so communication builds trust
