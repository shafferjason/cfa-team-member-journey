# Mastery Thresholds & Evaluation Framework

## Rating Scale

Four levels, observable, no ambiguity:

| Level | Label | What It Means |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| 1 | **Not Yet** | Hasn't demonstrated this behavior, or only with heavy prompting |
| 2 | **Emerging** | Shows it sometimes, inconsistent, tends to drop it under pressure |
| 3 | **Consistent** | Demonstrates reliably — including during rush, conflict, or stress |
| 4 | **Models** | Exemplifies it naturally and helps others develop it |

**Graduation threshold: Level 3 (Consistent) on every competency at your current tier.**

Level 4 is not required to advance — it's the mark of someone who's not just ready for the next tier but is already lifting others. That's your signal for high-potential identification.

---

## Floor-Embedded Development (Critical Design Decision)

### The Reality
Team Members and Trainers are fully engaged in operations. There is no classroom time, no desk time, no time pulled from the floor for development activities.

### The Principle
**The development IS the work.** The floor is the classroom.

### What This Means in Practice

**What goes away (for TM and Trainer tiers):**
- No assigned reading
- No homework
- No separate "development time" pulled from operations

**What stays (because it already happens):**
- Mentors/Trainers observing during normal shifts
- Brief coaching moments on the floor (this is literally TL Expectation #3: "Coach in the moment")
- Short conversations during natural pauses — pre-shift huddle, break room, end-of-shift
- 1-on-1s that already exist in the rhythm

### Development Method by Tier

| Tier | How They Develop | Added Time |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| **Team Member** | Floor observation + in-the-moment coaching + existing 1-on-1s | None |
| **Trainer** | Same as above + brief debrief after training sessions they lead | ~5 min/session |
| **Team Leader** | Above + a short resource before each 1-on-1 (article, chapter, video) + deliberate practice goals | 15-20 min/month |
| **Asst Director** | Structured mentoring with Operator + assigned reading + project-based learning | More significant — role has off-floor time |
| **Director** | Operator coaching + peer learning + self-directed development | Built into the role |
| **Operator** | Coach relationship + peer network + self-directed | Built into the role |

Structured reading, homework, and development resources kick in at **Team Leader and above** — where the role includes off-floor time and the expectation of self-development.

## How Leaders Develop Competencies on the Floor

The leader's job isn't to create a training program. It's to **notice, name, and expect** — consistently, every shift.

**Notice it:** Pay attention to when the behavior shows up and when it's absent.
**Name it:** Call it out in the moment — positive or corrective.
**Expect it:** Hold the standard. Don't let it slide just because it's a busy shift.

Example for Caring (TM level):
- See it: *"The way you handled that guest — that's exactly what we're about."*
- Missing it: *"That guest needed a moment from you. What did you notice?"*
- At the 1-on-1: *"I've noticed you're really growing in how you connect with guests. Here's where I'd like to see you push further."*

## Evaluation Structure

### Who Evaluates Whom

| Tier Being Evaluated | Evaluated By |
|---------------------|-------------|
| Team Member | Trainer or Team Leader |
| Trainer | Team Leader or Director |
| Team Leader | Director |
| Asst Director | Director or Operator |
| Director | Operator |

### Evaluation Rhythm

- **Ongoing:** In-the-moment coaching (daily, as it happens)
- **Monthly:** Competency touched on during existing 1-on-1 conversations
- **Midpoint:** Formal progress check at the halfway point of the runway (not pass/fail — a development conversation)
- **Graduation:** Formal evaluation at or after the runway minimum to determine readiness to advance

### Self-Assessment Component

At each formal check-in, the team member rates themselves on the same 4-level scale. Gaps between self-assessment and leader assessment become coaching conversations. This builds self-awareness — itself a leadership skill.

## Team Member Tier — Fully Defined

### Competency: Caring

**Observable behaviors — what a leader watches for:**
- Greets guests with genuine warmth, not a scripted line
- Notices when a teammate is struggling and offers help without being asked
- Shows patience with confused or difficult guests, even during rush
- Treats every interaction — guest or team — as if it matters

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Goes through the motions. Greeting feels scripted. Doesn't notice teammates or guests who need attention.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Shows genuine care when things are calm, but loses it during rush or when stressed. Inconsistent.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Maintains warmth and awareness even under pressure. Teammates and guests feel it reliably. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Other team members notice and comment on it. New hires naturally look to this person for how to treat people.

### Competency: Coachability

**Observable behaviors:**
- Receives feedback without defensiveness or excuses
- Applies feedback visibly — you can see the change in the next shift
- Asks questions to understand, not to argue
- Seeks feedback proactively — doesn't wait for it to come to them

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Gets defensive, shuts down, or agrees in the moment but nothing changes.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Accepts feedback gracefully but application is spotty. Needs reminding.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Actively seeks feedback. Applies it. You can see growth week over week. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Asks for feedback regularly. Other team members notice their growth and are inspired by it.

## Trainer Tier

### Competency: Teamwork

*What it means at this level:* You create an environment where the people around you can do their best work. As a Trainer, this shows up most clearly in how you set up trainees and teammates for success — not doing it for them, but making it easier for them to succeed.

**Observable behaviors:**
- Creates a calm, supportive environment during training — trainee feels safe to make mistakes
- Shares knowledge freely with the whole team, not just assigned trainees
- Steps back and lets the trainee try, even when it would be faster to do it yourself
- Celebrates others' wins publicly — points out when teammates do something well

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Hoards knowledge or does the work themselves because it's faster. Trainee feels like they're in the way.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Supportive in calm moments but takes over when it gets busy. Shares knowledge when asked but doesn't proactively teach.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Trainee feels set up for success even during rush. Actively shares leadership — trusts others to execute. Team is better when this person is on the shift. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Other trainers look to this person for how to train well. Creates a ripple effect — people they've trained go on to train others the same way.

### Competency: Caring (Deepened)

*How this differs from TM-level Caring:* At the TM level, Caring was about warmth toward guests and teammates — treating every interaction as if it matters. At the Trainer level, it deepens from "how I treat people" to "how I invest in the person I'm developing." It's relational depth, not just relational warmth.

**Observable behaviors:**
- Checks in on how the trainee is *feeling*, not just how they're *performing*
- Adjusts their teaching approach when someone isn't getting it — without frustration or impatience
- Notices when a trainee is overwhelmed and scales back before being asked
- Remembers personal details — knows what matters to the people they're developing

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Focuses only on task completion. Doesn't notice or respond to the trainee's emotional state. Training feels mechanical.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Shows care when things are going well, but gets frustrated when the trainee struggles. Adjusts approach sometimes but not consistently.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Trainees feel genuinely cared for — not just trained. This person adapts naturally to each individual. You can see the trainee's confidence grow shift over shift. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Former trainees stay connected to this person long after training ends. They're the one new hires ask to work with. Trainees don't just learn the job — they catch the culture.

## Team Leader Tier

### Competency: Interpersonal

*What it means at this level:* When conflict happens — between team members, with a guest, with a leader above you — you don't get defensive or blame. You own your part, listen first, and seek to understand before responding. This is the foundation for every hard conversation a leader will ever have.

**Observable behaviors:**
- When something goes wrong on their shift, asks "what's my part in this?" before pointing elsewhere
- In disagreements, restates the other person's position accurately before giving their own
- Receives criticism from Directors without shutting down or getting defensive
- When two team members are in conflict, listens to both sides before acting

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Blames others when things go wrong. Gets defensive when challenged. Avoids conflict or escalates it.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Owns their part when things are calm, but defaults to defensiveness under pressure. Listens, but you can tell they're waiting to talk, not trying to understand.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Owns mistakes openly — the team sees it. Actively seeks to understand the other side in any disagreement. Team members feel heard when they bring issues to this person. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Conflict actually gets *resolved* around this person, not just managed. Others come to them when they need a fair perspective. Sets the standard for how disagreements are handled on the team.

### Competency: Courage

*What it means at this level:* You address problems directly rather than avoiding them. When standards are slipping, you say something. When someone needs corrective feedback, you give it — in the moment, not after the shift. Maps directly to TL Expectation #2: "Own the standard in real time." Courage isn't about being harsh. It's about caring enough to be honest.

**Observable behaviors:**
- Addresses standard drift as it happens — doesn't wait for end of shift or hope someone else notices
- Gives corrective feedback directly to the person, not through a third party or behind their back
- Brings up uncomfortable topics with their Director rather than letting issues fester
- Holds the same standard for friends on the team as everyone else

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Avoids confrontation. Lets standards slide to keep the peace. Talks about problems to others but not to the person involved.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Addresses easy issues but avoids the hard ones — especially with friends or more experienced team members. Sometimes waits too long and the moment passes.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Addresses issues in real time regardless of who's involved. Does it with respect and directness — the person receiving it doesn't feel attacked, but they know the standard. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): The team's standards are visibly higher when this person leads the shift. They've earned the trust to give hard feedback because everyone knows it comes from care, not ego.

### Competency: Composure

*What it means at this level:* Your energy sets the tone. During rush, a guest complaint, a call-out, an equipment failure — you stay calm. The team looks at you and feels reassured rather than anxious. Maps directly to TL Expectation #1: "Stay present over productive." You can't stay present if you're spiraling.

**Observable behaviors:**
- During rush or crisis, voice stays steady, movements stay deliberate — doesn't rush or bark orders
- When something goes wrong (wrong order, angry guest, short-staffed), responds with a plan rather than panic
- Doesn't transfer their stress to the team — the team can't tell from their demeanor how bad it actually is
- After a difficult moment, resets quickly and re-engages rather than staying rattled

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Visibly stressed during rush. Snaps at team members or gets frantic. The team can feel the anxiety radiating off them.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Holds composure in routine pressure but loses it during real crises — unexpected situations, angry guests, multiple problems at once. Recovery takes too long.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Calm is the default, even when things go sideways. Team members feel steadied by this person's presence during difficult shifts. Recovers quickly from setbacks. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): The entire shift runs differently when this person is leading. Their composure is contagious — the team performs better under pressure because of the tone this person sets. This is the person you want leading your hardest shifts.

## Asst Director Tier

### Competency: Mentoring

*What it means at this level:* You shift from coaching in the moment (Trainer/Team Leader skill) to thinking about someone's development arc over time. You're not just helping people do today's job better — you're helping them become the leader they could be six months from now. Bridge to Director Expectation #3: "Develop leaders who create the experience."

**Observable behaviors:**
- Has an intentional development focus for each leader they work with — not just "good job" or "fix this," but a direction they're helping the person grow toward
- Gives feedback that's about the person's growth, not just the task — "Here's why this matters for where you're headed" vs. just "Do it this way"
- Notices strengths in people and puts them in positions to use those strengths
- Initiates development conversations — doesn't wait for the formal review to talk about growth

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Focuses only on operational output. Doesn't think about the people below them as developing leaders. Feedback is purely task-corrective.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Has good intentions about developing others but it's inconsistent. Does it when things are calm but drops it when operational pressure hits. Development conversations feel generic.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Each leader under them can articulate what they're working on and why. Development isn't a separate activity — it's woven into how this person leads. You can see their people growing. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Has a track record — people they've mentored have advanced and succeeded. Known across the restaurant as someone who builds leaders. Directors want their emerging leaders to spend time under this person.

### Competency: Integrity

*What it means at this level:* Your decisions about staffing, scheduling, and systems reflect the vision and values — even when it's inconvenient. You don't cut corners on culture to hit a number. You don't play favorites in the schedule. Connects to Director Expectation #4: "Shape the culture through key moments" — because culture erodes fastest when leaders aren't consistent.

**Observable behaviors:**
- Scheduling decisions are fair and transparent — the team trusts that the process isn't rigged
- Holds the same standard for themselves that they hold for others — doesn't exempt themselves from the rules
- When they make a mistake, they name it openly rather than covering or deflecting
- Makes the harder-but-right decision over the easier-but-wrong one, especially when no one would notice the difference

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Says the right things but actions don't match. Team members sense inconsistency between what's said and what's done. Plays favorites or makes exceptions that erode trust.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Consistent when things are easy, but bends under pressure — takes shortcuts, makes exceptions for convenience, avoids the harder right path. Intentions are good but follow-through is uneven.
- Level 3 (Consistent): The team trusts this person's word completely. What they say matches what they do, even in the hard moments. Decisions are explainable and defensible because they're rooted in values, not convenience. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Their integrity is a reference point for the team — "What would [name] do?" Others hold themselves to a higher standard because of this person's example.

### Competency: Decisions

*What it means at this level:* You're handling strategic operations — staffing levels, schedule structure, system design. These decisions can't wait. Indecision at this level creates drift that ripples through every shift. Connects to Director Expectation #1: "Remove friction by building better systems."

**Observable behaviors:**
- Makes staffing and scheduling decisions without excessive deliberation or needing to be pushed
- When faced with two imperfect options, picks one and commits rather than stalling
- Communicates decisions clearly so the team isn't left guessing
- When a decision turns out wrong, adjusts quickly rather than defending a bad call

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Avoids making decisions or constantly defers to the Director. Things that should take a day take a week. The team feels like nothing gets resolved.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Makes routine decisions well but freezes on the tough ones — the ones where someone might be unhappy or the answer isn't clear. Sometimes makes decisions but doesn't communicate them, leaving the team confused.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Makes timely decisions, including the unpopular ones. Communicates the "why" clearly. When they get it wrong, they own it and adjust fast. The team feels like things keep moving under this person's leadership. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Decision-making is a strength the whole team benefits from. They've developed a reputation for sound judgment. Directors trust them to make calls without checking in first.

### Competency: Results

*What it means at this level:* You're accountable for outcomes, not just effort. The systems you build either work or they don't. Good intentions don't count — the scoreboard matters. This is also where continuous improvement lives.

**Observable behaviors:**
- Sets clear targets for the systems they own and tracks whether they're hitting them
- When something isn't working, doesn't just identify the problem — takes action to fix it
- Pursues improvement proactively rather than waiting for a Director to point out what's broken
- Follows through — projects and initiatives get finished, not just started

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Busy but not productive. Works hard but can't point to measurable outcomes. Starts initiatives but doesn't finish them. Waits for direction rather than driving toward results.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Delivers results when given clear direction but doesn't self-initiate. Hits targets when the path is obvious but struggles when they need to figure out the "how" themselves.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Owns their outcomes. Can point to specific systems they've built or improved and the measurable impact. Drives continuous improvement without being asked. Directors don't have to follow up — things get done. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): The systems they've built run without them. Their results speak for themselves and become the standard others try to replicate. They see opportunities before anyone else and act on them.

## Director Tier

### Competency: Vision

*What it means at this level:* You articulate where your team or function is headed — and people follow because they understand and believe in the direction. Can your Team Leaders explain what you're building and why? If they can, your vision is working. Connects to Director Expectations #2 (see ahead) and #5 (communicate clearly and create alignment).

**Observable behaviors:**
- Their team or function has a clear direction that people can articulate — not just the Director, but the leaders under them
- Translates the restaurant's vision ("effortless experiences through intentional care") into specific, actionable terms for their area
- When making decisions, connects them back to where they're headed — people understand the "why" behind the "what"
- Inspires buy-in rather than just compliance — people want to follow the direction, not just obey it

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Manages tasks but doesn't set direction. The team works shift to shift without a sense of where they're building toward. Couldn't articulate a vision for their area if asked.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Has a vision internally but hasn't translated it for the team. Makes good decisions individually but people don't see the thread connecting them. Team Leaders follow instructions but don't carry the vision themselves.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Their team knows where they're going and why. Team Leaders can explain the direction to their own team members without the Director present. Decisions feel coherent over time — there's a clear trajectory. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Their vision energizes people. Team members actively want to be part of what this Director is building. Other Directors look to this person's clarity as a model for their own communication. The vision survives their absence — the team keeps building toward it even when the Director isn't there.

### Competency: Strategy

*What it means at this level:* You don't just see where to go — you design how to get there. You think in systems, not tasks. When something isn't working, you don't just fix the symptom — you redesign the process. Connects to Director Expectations #1 (remove friction by building better systems) and #2 (see ahead and prepare the organization).

**Observable behaviors:**
- Builds systems that solve recurring problems permanently rather than patching the same issue repeatedly
- Thinks ahead — forecasts staffing needs, training gaps, seasonal shifts before they become crises
- When proposing a change, has thought through second-order effects — "If we do this, what else does it impact?"
- Designs workflows with clear ownership so nothing falls through the cracks

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Reactive — fights the same fires repeatedly because the underlying systems aren't designed. Proposes solutions without thinking through consequences. Team is always in recovery mode.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Good at solving problems but still mostly reactive. Builds some systems but they're fragile — they work when everything goes right but break under pressure. Doesn't consistently think ahead.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Recurring problems get solved permanently because this person builds systems, not just fixes. Anticipates needs before they're urgent. Their area of the restaurant runs more smoothly over time, not just shift to shift. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Their systems become the template for other Directors. They think multiple moves ahead — the organization is prepared for what's coming because this person saw it first. Other Directors and the Operator seek their input on strategic decisions.

### Competency: Balance

*What it means at this level:* A Director who burns out can't lead. This is a leadership competency, not a wellness perk. Balance means knowing what only you can do, protecting your energy for those things, and trusting your leaders to handle the rest. If Directors model sustainability, the whole team learns that you can lead well without destroying yourself.

**Observable behaviors:**
- Delegates effectively — doesn't try to be involved in everything or hold on to tasks their leaders can handle
- Has a rhythm to their work — their leaders know when and how to reach them, rather than everything being reactive and chaotic
- Shows up with energy and presence for the moments that matter (openings, transitions, peaks) because they've managed their capacity
- Makes time for reflection and development, not just execution — is still growing, not just grinding

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Always busy but burning out. Can't delegate — personally involved in everything. No work rhythm, just constant reaction. Team sees a leader who's exhausted, and they assume that's what leadership requires.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Understands the importance of balance intellectually but can't consistently practice it. Delegates sometimes but takes things back when stressed. Has periods of sustainability followed by stretches of burnout.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Has a sustainable rhythm. Delegates with trust. Shows up with energy when it matters most. Their leaders feel empowered rather than micromanaged. Still investing in their own growth. The team sees a leader who's effective AND sustainable. **<-- Graduation threshold**
- Level 4 (Models): Their sustainability is contagious — the leaders under them learn to manage their own capacity because of this person's example. Has designed their role around what only they can do. White space isn't wasted — it's where their best strategic thinking happens. This is the leader who's still effective and energized years into the role.

## Operator Tier

*Note: The Operator tier is different. There's no graduation — it's an ongoing pursuit. There's no leader above doing floor observations. Evaluation comes from the coach, self-reflection, and the health of the organization. These competencies play out over months and years, not shifts.*

### Competency: Stewardship

*What it means at this level:* You are a faithful owner of what's been entrusted to you — the people, the culture, the brand, the community presence. The 6 essential jobs that only you can do, scheduled into anchored blocks. Encompasses all 5 CFA Critical Success Factors held as a single responsibility.

**Observable indicators:**
- The essential work that only the Operator can do is identified, scheduled, and protected — not squeezed in reactively
- Financial health of the business is actively managed — not just reviewed when problems surface
- Invests back into the business (people, equipment, environment) rather than extracting maximum short-term return
- Makes decisions as an owner, not a manager — weighs long-term health over short-term convenience

**Self-assessment questions:**
- If I look at my last month, did I complete every essential anchor? If not, what pulled me away?
- Am I investing in the areas that will matter in 3 years, or just managing what's in front of me this week?
- Would the person who entrusted this business to me be proud of how I'm tending to it?
- Am I taking care of the financial health in a way that's sustainable, not just profitable?

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): Reactive — the week ends and you're not sure what you accomplished. Essential work gets crowded out by fires. Business decisions are short-term focused.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Knows what the essential work is but can't protect it consistently. Good months and bad months. Financial stewardship is present but inconsistent.
- Level 3 (Consistent): Essential anchors are scheduled and completed reliably. The business is healthy across all 5 CSFs. Decisions reflect long-term ownership, not short-term management. You could account for how you spent your time and feel good about it.
- Level 4 (Models): The business thrives even during disruption because the foundations are so well-tended. Other operators look to your stewardship practices as a model. Your coach sees sustained, intentional ownership over years, not just months.

### Competency: Scalability

*What it means at this level:* Your leadership multiplies without your presence. You've built leaders who build leaders. The restaurant runs — and the culture holds — when you're not there. Coach's test: "If you had your least available, least present month, would you still be a good steward?"

**Observable indicators:**
- Can take a week away and the restaurant runs well — not just survives, but runs to standard
- Directors make sound decisions without needing Operator input on every issue
- The leadership pipeline is producing — there's always someone ready or nearly ready for the next role
- Problems get solved at the level where they occur rather than escalating to the Operator by default

**Self-assessment questions:**
- If I were unavailable for a full week with no notice, what would break? That's my scalability gap.
- Are my Directors making decisions, or waiting for mine?
- Is there a leader ready to step into every critical role if someone left tomorrow?
- Am I holding onto things because only I can do them, or because I haven't invested in building someone else's ability to do them?

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): The restaurant can't function without you. You're involved in every decision. When you're gone, standards slip, problems pile up, and you come back to a mess.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Some areas run well without you, but others collapse. Some Directors operate independently, others still need constant guidance. The pipeline has gaps — if a key leader left, there's no one ready.
- Level 3 (Consistent): The restaurant runs to standard in your absence. Directors lead with confidence and sound judgment. The pipeline is healthy — there's depth at every tier. You spend your time on what only you can do because everything else is covered.
- Level 4 (Models): Your organization runs so well that it's hard to tell whether you were there this week or not — and that's the highest compliment. You've built leaders who are building leaders. The system perpetuates itself.

### Competency: Legacy

*What it means at this level:* The culture you're shaping outlasts any individual — including you. The hospitality ethos, the development paths, the "how we made people feel." What your 11-year team member described: "Every year just keeps getting better." Legacy is building something that keeps getting better even after you stop touching it.

**Observable indicators:**
- Team members who leave speak positively about their experience — the alumni path is real, not just a concept
- The culture is consistent regardless of who's leading a particular shift — it's embedded in the organization, not dependent on one personality
- Values and standards are taught and caught — new hires absorb them from the environment, not just from a handbook
- Long-tenured team members stay because they want to, not because they have to

**Self-assessment questions:**
- If I stepped away permanently, would the culture I've built survive the transition? For how long?
- Do people leave here better than they arrived — not just as employees, but as people?
- Are my values and the restaurant's values living in the team, or do they live only in me?
- Twenty years from now, what will the people who worked here remember about this place?

**Scoring:**
- Level 1 (Not Yet): The culture depends on your personal presence. When you're engaged, things are great. When you're not, it drifts. People are loyal to you personally, not to what you've built.
- Level 2 (Emerging): Pockets of strong culture exist — certain teams, certain Directors carry it. But it's inconsistent across the restaurant. Some team members get the experience, others don't.
- Level 3 (Consistent): The culture is the culture — it doesn't depend on who's working that shift. Alumni speak well of the organization. Team members at every level can articulate the values and live them. The development framework is producing people who carry it forward.
- Level 4 (Models): People talk about working here the way they talk about formative life experiences. Your leaders carry the culture into whatever they do next — other restaurants, other careers, other communities. What you've built has become bigger than you.

## Connection to Existing Frameworks

### Mid-Shift Self-Check (for TL graduation criteria)
The nine reflective questions from the Vision & Leadership Directive can serve as a practical readiness test for the Team Leader → Team Leader transition. A Team Leader candidate who can consistently answer those questions well is demonstrating the competencies at Level 3.

### Own It vs. Escalate (for TL graduation criteria)
Sound judgment on when to own a decision versus escalate it demonstrates the Courage, Composure, and Interpersonal competencies in action. This can be formally assessed as part of the graduation evaluation.
